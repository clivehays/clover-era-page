A CFO told me last month his company lost $4.2 million to turnover last year.

I asked what he was doing about it.

"We have someone for that. Talk to HR."

I did. The Head of People showed me their budget: $340,000. Annual surveys. A recognition platform with 11% adoption. Quarterly pulse checks that leadership reads once then files.

I asked if she had the authority to change comp bands when someone's underpaid. No.

If she could restructure teams when a manager's underwater. No.

If she could override a promotion decision when someone's been passed over twice. No.

"So what can you do?"

"I can measure how people feel about the things I can't change."

That's the conversation that happens at most companies. The person who owns the P&L delegates a $4 million problem to someone with a $340,000 budget and no authority to touch the levers that actually matter.

Then leadership acts shocked when turnover stays high.

Where the Money Actually Goes

Here's what most CFOs miss: turnover doesn't show up as a line item called "turnover cost."

It's scattered.

Recruiting fees get filed under talent acquisition. Overtime covering gaps shows up in payroll. Lost deals appear as sales slippage. Consultant hours covering knowledge gaps land in professional services. Projects that slip because the technical lead left get blamed on "capacity issues."

By the time you reconcile it, the damage is two quarters old and attributed to something else.

Finance teams track turnover rate—a lagging percentage that feels manageable at 8%.

Nobody tracks turnover cost—the real-time dollar impact that's actually running at $4.2 million.

Here's what that $4.2 million actually represents:

Recruiting fees: $15K-$25K per senior hire, paid to agencies because internal recruiting can't keep up with the backfill demand.

Ramp time productivity gap: A $120K employee who leaves in March won't be fully replaced until August. That's five months of reduced output. The new person operating at 40%, then 60%, then 80% while they learn the systems, the clients, the unwritten rules.

Overtime covering gaps: The team that's down a person doesn't stop working. They absorb the load. That's overtime, comp time, or burnout that leads to the next departure.

Projects that slip: The technical lead who walked out the door was three weeks from launching the feature that would close the enterprise deal. Now it's pushed right. The deal slips. Revenue moves to next quarter.

Client relationships rebuilt: Your account manager left. The client doesn't know the new person. Trust resets to zero. The renewal that was automatic is now at risk.

Manager time consumed: Your engineering director spends 40 hours interviewing replacements, 20 hours onboarding, 60 hours covering gaps in knowledge transfer. That's 120 hours of leadership capacity diverted from building product to backfilling roles.

Cascade effect: One departure triggers two more. The person who left was the glue holding a team together. Now the other senior engineer is fielding recruiter calls. The departure you're replacing in May causes the departures you'll be replacing in August.

This isn't headcount. This is working capital walking out the door.

The person tracking working capital—the CFO—delegated it to someone who can't access the levers that prevent it.

Run the Numbers

The Authority Mismatch

HR can measure engagement. They can't change comp bands.

HR can run pulse surveys. They can't restructure teams when a manager's drowning.

HR can document flight risk. They can't override a promotion decision when someone's been passed over twice.

HR can offer exit interviews. By then the decision was made 67 days ago and it's too late to recover the knowledge walking out.

The levers that actually prevent turnover—manager behavior, workload distribution, comp decisions, promotion paths—don't roll up to HR. They roll up to operations.

Manager behavior: Does this manager create psychological safety or do people on their team shut down? That's a performance issue. Operations.

Workload distribution: Is this team underwater because two roles are unfilled and nobody redistributed the work? That's capacity planning. Operations.

Comp decisions: Is this person underpaid by $15K relative to market and nobody caught it until they had an offer? That's compensation strategy. Finance and operations.

Promotion paths: Has this person been passed over twice while watching less qualified people get promoted? That's succession planning. Operations.

The $4.2 million problem sits at the intersection of people, process, and margin.

The person you handed it to controls none of those.

You didn't delegate the problem. You just made sure nobody with power was accountable for solving it.

Why Finance Teams Miss It

CFOs obsess over cost of goods sold. They track customer acquisition cost down to the dollar. They model gross margin by product line.

Then they treat turnover like it's a percentage in a quarterly board deck.

Here's why:

It doesn't appear as "turnover cost" on the P&L. It's disguised as a dozen other line items, none of which get tagged with the root cause.

It's calculated wrong. Most finance teams use 1.5x salary as the replacement cost. A $91,000 employee = $136,500 to replace. That math ignores ramp time, lost productivity, cascade risk, and client impact. The actual cost is closer to 4x. That same $91,000 employee costs $366,000 when you account for what actually happens.

It's measured as a lagging indicator. Turnover rate tells you what happened last quarter. It doesn't tell you what's about to happen. The person who decided to leave in January won't resign until March. You're managing the problem 67 days after it became unfixable.

Nobody owns the forward-looking number. CAC has a target. Gross margin has a target. Turnover cost? There's no budget line, no monthly variance report, no executive owner.

The result: a $4 million problem that gets treated like an HR initiative instead of a margin leak.

What Changes When CFOs Own It

I watched this shift happen with the CFO I opened with.

He stopped delegating turnover to HR. He started tracking it the same way he tracks COGS.

Here's what changed:

Turnover cost became a line item. It sits in his finance dashboard next to revenue and gross margin. Updated monthly. Reviewed in the executive team meeting. Owned by him.

Prevention budget got compared to replacement cost. 15 minutes of manager coaching per week costs $12,000 annually in manager time. Preventing one $120K departure saves $480K in actual turnover cost. That's a 40:1 ROI. When he saw the math that way, manager enablement stopped being a "nice to have" and became operational infrastructure.

Manager behavior became a performance metric. Managers who generate turnover now carry that cost on their scorecard. A manager who lost three people in six months isn't just "having team issues". They're responsible for $1.2 million in margin erosion. That reframes the conversation.

Flight risk became a forward-looking financial metric. Instead of tracking turnover rate (lagging), they track how many people are showing Already Gone signals (leading). That's the number that predicts the cost coming in 60-90 days. Now they have time to intervene.

The question shifted. It's no longer "how do people feel?" It's "what's the cost of losing this person and what does prevention cost?" When you frame it that way, the ROI is absurd. Spending $24K on manager enablement to prevent $2-5 million in turnover isn't a people investment. It's a margin decision.

He hasn't handed it back to HR. He shouldn't. It's a $4 million line item.

He owns it.
